Tuition Assistance

What Is Tuition Assistance and How Does It Work?

A quick overview of what Tuition Assistance will and will not pay for, how the caps work, why command approval matters, and what happens if you drop or fail a class.

The Short Answer

Tuition Assistance (TA) is a Department of Defense program that pays most of your tuition only for approved off‑duty college courses while you are in service.

In most branches, the standard cap is up to 250 dollars per semester hour and up to 4,500 dollars per fiscal year, with service‑specific limits on how many credits you can take per year and over your career layered on top. You still have to get command or supervisor approval in your service’s portal before funding is locked in, and if you fail or withdraw from a class you can be required to pay TA back unless you qualify for a waiver.

What TA Will Pay For

  • Tuition charges for for‑credit college courses (associate, bachelor’s, master’s) that are part of an approved degree or credential plan.
  • Classes at accredited schools that have a signed DoD Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) on file.
  • Courses taken off duty, whether in person or fully online.

What TA Will NOT Pay For

  • Fees of any kind: application, registration, technology, lab, parking, graduation, or similar charges.
  • Books, e‑books, supplies, or equipment.
  • Non‑credit or recreational classes, or repeat attempts of a course you already passed using TA.

How the Money Caps Actually Work

The headline TA numbers sound simple, but it helps to know what they look like in practice.

  • Per‑credit cap. TA pays up to 250 dollars per semester hour. If your school charges more than that, TA still pays only up to the cap and you cover the difference with other aid or out of pocket.
  • Annual cap. TA stops at 4,500 dollars per fiscal year (1 October–30 September). Once you hit that ceiling, you are done for that year even if you still have classes you want to take.
  • Service limits. Each branch also sets its own limits on total semester hours per year and total lifetime usage, often broken out by degree level (for example, a maximum number of credits toward a bachelor’s and a separate limit toward a master’s).

Command Approval

TA is not automatic. You request it through your service’s education portal (ArmyIgnitED, MyNavy Education/WebTA, AFVEC, and so on), and your chain of command has to sign off before the education office releases any funds.

No approval means no TA payment, even if you were otherwise eligible. When your supervisor or commanding officer reviews your request, they are essentially checking that you meet minimum time‑in‑service requirements, have completed initial training, are in good standing on fitness and conduct, and that the class schedule will not interfere with mission requirements.

If You Drop or Fail

This is where many people get surprised. TA is not “free” in the sense that you can walk away with no consequences if a class goes badly.

  • Recoupment is the default. You can expect to owe money back if you earn a non‑passing grade (commonly a D or F for undergraduate courses, a C or worse in many graduate programs, or an unresolved “I” for incomplete) or if you withdraw after the school’s refund period for personal reasons.
  • Unearned TA is returned first. When you withdraw early in a term, your school is required to return the “unearned” portion of TA funds to your branch. Any remaining balance on your student account then becomes your personal bill with the school.
  • Waivers exist but are not automatic. You can request a waiver of recoupment if the withdrawal or failure was caused by deployment, PCS, serious medical issues, or other duty‑related situations—but you will need documentation and you must follow your service’s specific waiver process and deadlines.

Fast Rules of Thumb

  • Start early. Submit TA requests well before classes start so your chain of command and the education office have time to review and approve everything without rushing.
  • Treat TA like a contract. Keep your grades solid and show up for the work. Poor performance can cost you money in recoupment and can also affect your eligibility for TA in future terms.
  • Use TA first, GI Bill later. In most cases, it is smarter to use TA while you are on active duty and save your GI Bill for after separation or for a period when you can attend school full time.
