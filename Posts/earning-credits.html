Earning Credits While You Serve: CLEP, DSST, and JST

Before you spend a single dollar of Tuition Assistance or a single month of GI Bill entitlement, it is worth finding out how many college credits you may already have sitting on the table.

The Short Answer

Before you spend a single dollar of Tuition Assistance or a single month of GI Bill entitlement, it is worth finding out how many college credits you may already have sitting on the table. Between your military training, occupational experience, and the government-funded exams available to you at no cost, it is entirely possible to walk into a degree program with a semester or more of credit already counted. This guide breaks down the three tools, JST, CLEP, and DSST, explains what each one does and how to use it strategically, and points you toward a few other options that are not government-run but are worth knowing about.

Your JST: Start Here

The Joint Services Transcript is an official academic record that documents your military training, occupational experience, and any exam scores you have accumulated during your time in service. It is maintained through DANTES and is available to all active duty, reserve, and Guard members at no cost. When you submit it to a school's admissions office, they evaluate it against ACE (American Council on Education) recommendations and decide how many credits to award.

What those credits tend to cover:

  • Most JST credits are applied as general electives, not core requirements. That is the pattern for the majority of service members.
  • Direct course equivalencies happen but are less common and depend heavily on your rating or MOS and the degree you are pursuing.
  • Some schools are far more generous than others. Military-friendly schools with a signed DoD MOU tend to award more credit and apply it more flexibly.

One estimate puts the average awarded credit from JST at roughly 33% of the hours needed for a bachelor's degree, though results vary widely by branch, rating, and school. (VA.gov)

How to find out what you would actually get

To get a real number, you have to go through the school's admissions or transfer credit office directly. In my experience, most schools will do a preliminary credit evaluation before you formally enroll. You submit the JST, they review it, and they give you an unofficial estimate of what would transfer and how it would apply to your program. Some schools have online transfer credit tools where you can upload documents and get an estimate without talking to anyone. Others require you to go through a formal application first before they will evaluate anything. It is worth asking if the school can do a preliminary JST evaluation before you commit to enrolling.

The strategic move: Pull your JST before you apply anywhere. It is free, it is immediate, and it tells you what you are already working with. Once you know roughly how many credits you are bringing in and how a school applies them, you can compare schools based on how they handle military transfer credit and not just tuition rates or reputation.

CLEP: Test Out of What You Already Know

CLEP (College-Level Examination Program) is run by the College Board and offers over 30 exams covering introductory-level college subjects. Each exam takes about 90 minutes and is computer-based. Pass it and you earn 3 to 12 college credits depending on the subject.

What you need to know:

  • DANTES funds your first attempt only on each exam title. If you do not pass, you must wait three months and pay the exam fee yourself to retake it. DANTES will not cover a second attempt on the same title. (College Board) (DANTES.mil)
  • Over 2,900 colleges accept CLEP credit. Most military-friendly schools that have signed the DoD MOU are among them, but always verify before you test.
  • CLEP exams tend to cover general education requirements: history, sociology, psychology, composition, mathematics, sciences. For many service members, those are exactly the electives and core gen-ed courses standing between you and a degree.
  • Military Pass rates vary widely by subject. In FY24, College Composition (82%), Introductory Sociology (78%), and Natural Sciences (76%) were among the highest, while College Algebra (34%) and Chemistry (27%) were among the lowest. (DANTES.mil FY24 CLEP Pass Rates)

The strategic move: Look at your desired degree plan first, then identify which gen-ed requirements could be covered by CLEP. A few weekends of focused study can knock out 3 credits per exam. That is TA money and class time you keep in your pocket.

DSST: More Subjects, Including Upper-Level Options

DSST (DANTES Subject Standardized Tests) exams are managed by Prometric and cover more than 37 subjects across business, social sciences, math, humanities, physical sciences, and applied technology. Unlike CLEP, DSST includes some upper-division course equivalencies, which can be useful if you are further into a degree program or pursuing a business-related field.

What you need to know:

  • Like CLEP, DANTES funds your first attempt on each DSST exam title at no cost to you.
  • Each exam typically earns 3 credit hours. Over 1,500 colleges and universities accept DSST scores.
  • DSST is particularly useful for business, management, and technology-related degrees because the subject catalog maps well to those program requirements.
  • The average exam takes a few hours compared to 8 to 15 weeks in a classroom or online course.

The strategic move: If you are pursuing a business or management degree, look at the DSST catalog before enrolling in any electives or foundational business courses. Several of those can be tested out in an afternoon.

The Bottom Line

Before you apply for TA or touch your GI Bill, pull your JST and see what you already have. Then look at the CLEP and DSST catalogs and identify any exams that align with your degree requirements. Between those three tools alone, many service members walk into a program with enough existing credit to significantly shorten their timeline and reduce what they have to spend. Go into your degree with as many credits already on the books as you can, so you’re spending TA and GI Bill on the classes you actually need, not on stuff you’ve basically already done.
